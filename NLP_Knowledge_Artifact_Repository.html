Sustainability & Societal Impact

How NLP Can Contribute to Society and Sustainable Development

Language is the biggest barrier standing between people and the information, services, and opportunities that already exist. NLP shrinks that barrier. In a country like India, where over twenty languages are spoken by more than a million people each, most government forms, banking apps, and health advisories are still written in English or Hindi first. Techniques covered in this repository — tokenization, embeddings, and language models — are exactly what make it possible to translate a hospital discharge summary into Marathi or Konkani, or to let a farmer ask a chatbot a question in their own dialect and get an answer that makes sense. That is language accessibility and regional language technology working together, and it directly affects who gets to participate in a digital economy and who gets left out of it.

The same techniques support educational inclusion. Automatic summarization and simplification tools can take a dense textbook chapter and turn it into a shorter, easier version for a first-generation learner, without needing a human tutor available at every hour. Assistive technologies built on speech-to-text and text-to-speech pipelines give visually impaired or dyslexic students a way to consume the same material as everyone else, at their own pace. None of this needs a new invention — it needs the preprocessing and representation techniques in this repository applied thoughtfully to underserved languages and underserved learners, which are usually the last to receive investment.

At the level of governance, citizen services increasingly run through chatbots and automated grievance systems that rely on the same NLP stack: a citizen's complaint is tokenized, cleaned, classified, and routed to the right department in seconds instead of weeks. This reduces paperwork, cuts down the carbon and paper cost of manual processing, and makes public services measurably faster for people who cannot afford to take a day off work to visit an office. Taken together, the Week 1 techniques in this repository are not just an academic exercise — they are the ground floor of a more inclusive, more sustainable digital society, and understanding them is the first step toward building NLP responsibly rather than just building it.

Reflection — Chinmay Satam

Roll 58

Before this activity I treated stemming and lemmatization as basically the same thing. Building the comparison table forced me to actually run both on the same word list, and seeing stemming spit out "studi" while lemmatization correctly gave "study" made the difference click in a way no textbook definition had. Drawing the preprocessing pipeline was harder than expected — I kept forgetting where noise removal should sit relative to tokenization, and had to redo the diagram twice. The biggest takeaway for me is that preprocessing order actually changes results, it isn't just a checklist. Next time I'd want to test this pipeline on a messy, real dataset instead of clean textbook sentences.

Reflection — Suyog Satam

Roll 59

Working on TF-IDF and the embedding concepts showed me how much the field moved from just counting words to actually representing meaning. Writing the Word2Vec vs FastText comparison was the most useful part — I hadn't realized FastText's sub-word trick is exactly why it handles typos and rare words better, since I could reason through the mechanism instead of memorizing a bullet point. The hardest part was keeping every concept card short without losing accuracy; I had to rewrite a few definitions three or four times to compress them without them becoming vague. I'd like to eventually try training a small Word2Vec model myself to see these ideas in practice.
